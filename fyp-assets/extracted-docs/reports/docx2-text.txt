Abstract

This individual project proposal focuses on my primary role in the
inter-departmental Final Year Project: the design and fabrication of
components for a passive direct liquid fuel cell stack powered by
vanadium-based electrically rechargeable liquid fuel (e-fuel).

As a Mechanical Engineering student, I will lead the development of the

current collectors, and overall stack assembly, to enable efficient
power generation for an electric toy car prototype. This addresses
limitations of traditional batteries and fuel cells, such as long

and catalyst-free operation. My contributions will integrate with team
efforts on performance evaluation and toy car prototyping. The
methodology emphasizes mechanical design using SolidWorks, AI training
using Python, and toy car fabrication using sheet metal and 3D print.

2. Literature Review

Fuel cells convert chemical energy directly into electricity through
electrochemical reactions, offering high efficiency and low
environmental impact compared to internal combustion engines. A
comparative study of fuel cell technologies highlights that direct
liquid fuel cells (DLFCs) provide ease of fuel handling and high energy
density, surpassing gaseous hydrogen systems in portability. However,
traditional DLFCs using methanol or ethanol require noble catalysts and
yield limited performance.

Vanadium-based systems, particularly vanadium redox flow batteries
(VRFBs), have emerged as promising due to their scalability and long
cycle life. A comprehensive review of VRFBs emphasizes design
considerations like electrode compression (optimal at 25%) and
electrolyte flow rates (37.5-150 cm³/min), which enhance energy
efficiency up to 85% while addressing limitations such as capacity
fading through thermal treatments at 390-415°C. Operational conditions
in VRFBs, including electrolyte flow and membrane type, significantly
impact performance; for instance, anion exchange membranes yield higher
efficiency than Nafion but with greater fading.

Recent advancements focus on electrically rechargeable liquid fuels
(e-fuels) for DLFCs. A 2020 study demonstrated an e-fuel cell with a
catalyst-free graphite-felt anode and air cathode, achieving a peak
power density of 293 mW/cm² and 42.3% energy efficiency at room
temperature. This system uses vanadium ions in sulfuric acid, enabling
rechargeability and superior reactivity over alcohol. Passive designs
further simplify the system. A 2021 passive e-fuel cell reported 116.2
mW/cm² with over 350 hours of stability.

Key components include thermally treated graphite anodes, cathodes,
Nafion membranes, and vanadium electrolytes (1.0-1.6M V in 2-4M H₂SO₄).
Fabrication involves MEA assembly, and current collectors with optimized
open ratios. These insights guide my role in addressing fabrication
challenges like electrolyte stability and mechanical durability.

Incorporate serpentine or perforated flow fields in current collectors

Design the e-fuel tank from acrylic/PVC for safe storage (at least 20 mL
capacity for each cell), with injection ports. Ensure passive operation
via natural convection, utilize the vibration when driving to increase
convection.

The other factor I need to take into consideration when designing the
battery is the refilling process, the battery stack we will make will
require relatively high voltage and power, so to make sure every refill
only takes reasonable amount of time, we must take it into consideration
when designing the stack.

*Power for the cell is directly tied to active area, as it determines
the electrochemical reaction sites; voltage losses are minimized through
electrode treatments, but area scaling requires careful design. Larger
areas can indirectly reduce voltage due to increased ohmic losses
(resistance to ion/electron flow) or mass transport limitations if flow
distribution is uneven.

We are provided with a design done by previous FYP student for
reference; I noticed a few rooms for improvement with this design:

1.  The refuelling is extremely difficult on this design as the opening
    are very small. Affecting its scalability as it will become very
    difficult to refuel it after we increase the stack.

2.  The tank for the design is small, since e-fuel already have poor
    power density, the battery will not be able to last very long.

3.  It is easy to mess up the refuelling as the fuel filler are all at
    one side of the battery.

4.  It is also difficult to remove used fuel on this design.

5.  The fuel filler does not have a cover, which means the fuel may
    spill and corrode other parts.

So, for our design I will make sure the battery has an easy refuel
process, larger fuel tank combined with better utilization of car motion
for better convection. Fuel filler covers to prevent leakage, with clear
indication of cathode and anode.

7. Summary

This project addresses the limitations of traditional cells by utilizing
e-fuel, which combines the rapid refueling advantage of fuel cells with
superior reactivity and rechargeability, while eliminating the need for
expensive noble metal catalysts. The project will involve the design,
fabrication, and experimental optimization of the e-fuel cell.
Subsequently, the optimized fuel cell stack will be integrated with a
powertrain and electronic control systems to build a functional toy car
prototype capable of tracking and navigation.

The final outcome will be a demonstrable model that showcases e-fuel
cell technology as a viable and efficient pathway for future renewable
energy applications.
